About F# and fsharpConf

F# is an open-source, cross-platform, functional first programming language. F# makes it easy to solve complex problems with simple code. It integrates smoothly with existing .NET code bases. And... it makes coding fun again!

F# has an active open-source community building on a wide range of tools for web programming, data analytics, testing, cloud computing and much more. At fsharpConf, we want to bring some of the community together in a single-day online event!

The fsharpConf conference is organized by the F# community. The event is the third edition, following a successful fsharpConf in 2018 and 2016. It was originally inspired by dotNetConf and we are excited to be continuing this great tradition of virtual events!

Probabilistic Programming: The What, Why and How

f(x) = y, but y = 5. Whatâs the probability that x =1? Probabilistic programming is the idea of describing probabilistic models as programs, to then automatically infer how our beliefs about model parameters change given observed data. In recent years, probabilistic programming languages (such as Stan) have demonstrated the power of this approach by becoming the underlying tool behind numerous projects in social science, biology, genetics, astrophysics, and engineering.

But why havenât such languages been more widely adopted yet? In this talk, Maria will talk about the nuts and bolts of probabilistic programming languages, addressing the challenges behind making these languages general-purpose, automatic, and efficient. In addition, she will discuss how ideas from programming-language research can be adopted in probabilistic systems, and how F# has helped her in the endeavour of bridging the gap between statistical modelling and probabilistic inference.

Maria Gorinova

Maria is a PhD Student at the University of Edinburgh with broad interests range from programming languages and verification, to machine learning and human-computer interaction. More specifically, she's interested in probabilistic programming languages, and in exploring ways of applying program-analysis techniques to existing PPLs in order to improve usability of the language or efficiency of inference. During her PhD, she has been working on these topics in relation to the probabilistic programming languages Stan and Edward2.

Formatting F# source code

Having a good formatter for your source code seems like a well-established concept for many languages in these modern times. Prettier for JavaScript, for example, is a joy to work with. The formatting story for F# is a different one. There is a project called Fantomas, which was started in 2013 and sadly it lost its main contributor. Until it was rejuvenated in 2018 by a dynamic duo in the F# mentorship program. In this talk, I'd like to share my journey from my first pull request to become a maintainer of Fantomas. Discussing the technical aspects as well as the road to ownership.
